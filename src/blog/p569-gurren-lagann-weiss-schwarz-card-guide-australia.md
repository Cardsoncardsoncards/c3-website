# Gurren Lagann Weiss Schwarz Card Guide (Australia)

Gurren Lagann is one of the enduring classics of mecha anime, known for its over the top energy, its message about defying fate through sheer willpower, and the bond between Simon and Kamina that anchors the entire series. Despite being one of the more beloved mecha properties of its generation, Weiss Schwarz did not release its Gurren Lagann set until 2018, well over a decade after the original anime aired, which has given the set a genuinely nostalgic collector base from day one.

## The set at a glance

Gurren Lagann is a single stand-alone set in English, released 6 July 2018, with a total of 158 cards. It has not received a second English wave, so this remains the complete Gurren Lagann collection available on the platform. The set code is GL, and the release featured 100 base card types plus 22 parallel versions, packed 8 cards per pack, 20 packs per display.

The Booster Pack picks up from the Trial Deck+, covering scenes from the middle and later parts of the anime through to the end of its first season, giving collectors a genuine spread of moments from across the show's earlier arcs.

## Deck building around Team Dai-Gurren

The card pool supports several distinct deck strategies drawing directly from the anime's factions and themes. You can build a Team Dai-Gurren deck around the core cast, a Beastmen deck drawing on the show's antagonist faction, or an Anti-Spiral deck for the later story's cosmic scale threat. This range of viable archetypes from one set gives Gurren Lagann more build variety than many single release properties manage.

One of the set's most distinctive mechanics directly mirrors the anime's core visual gag, mechs stacking on top of each other mid battle. The boss card, Tengen Toppa Gurren Lagann, lets you attack, then pay a cost to bring another mech in and attack again, chaining the process to build one overwhelming combined attack, exactly like the escalating mech stacking sequences from the anime itself. It is one of the more thematically faithful translations of a source material's core identity into card game mechanics that Weiss Schwarz has produced.

## Chase cards and signature pulls

Every pack carries a chance of a signed card from the voice cast, including Tetsuya Kakihara as Simon, Katsuyuki Konishi as Kamina, Marina Inoue as Yoko, and Yukari Fukui as Nia. Display boxes included one of four exclusive PR cards. Signed Yoko cards in particular have shown up regularly in graded card sales, reflecting her enduring popularity as one of the series' most recognisable characters.

Because these signature cards were pulled from packs years after the original anime aired, in a franchise revival release rather than a same year tie in, they carry a distinct kind of scarcity value, being genuinely rare pulls for a beloved older series rather than a mass produced contemporary chase item.

## Why this property still resonates with collectors

Gurren Lagann's core message about pushing past perceived limits has kept the series culturally relevant well beyond its original broadcast run, and that lasting relevance shows up directly in ongoing demand for this card set. Unlike many older licensed sets that fade in interest once nostalgia alone is doing the work, Gurren Lagann benefits from a genuinely committed, still active fan community that continues collecting and discussing the franchise.

For Australian collectors, this makes Gurren Lagann a solid choice if you are building a collection around genuinely classic, influential anime rather than current seasonal releases. Demand here has proven durable rather than dependent on ongoing new content.

## Why a delayed release matters for this set

Most Weiss Schwarz sets release within a year or two of their source anime airing, riding the wave of contemporary popularity. Gurren Lagann's set arriving roughly a decade after the anime finished is unusual, and it changes how the set should be understood as a collectible. Rather than capturing a fanbase at peak initial excitement, this release specifically targeted an audience that had already proven, over many years, that they still cared about the franchise. That is a stronger signal of durable demand than an immediate same year release can offer, since the fans buying in 2018 were doing so purely out of ongoing love for the series rather than being caught up in a fresh release cycle.

For collectors thinking about long term value, this delayed release pattern is worth watching for in other franchises too. A set built around an older, previously unaddressed classic tends to attract a more committed buyer base than a set riding a brand new show's opening hype, even if the total buyer pool is smaller in absolute terms.

## What to check before buying

1. Confirm whether a listing is base rarity or a signed voice cast version before paying a premium, since Yoko and Simon signatures in particular carry a meaningful price gap.
2. Check condition carefully on older stock. As a set that is now several years old, some circulating copies show genuine wear from handling over time.
3. If building competitively, decide whether you want a Team Dai-Gurren, Beastmen, or Anti-Spiral focused deck before buying broadly, since the three archetypes draw on different parts of the card pool.
