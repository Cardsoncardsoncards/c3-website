# Chainsaw Man Weiss Schwarz Card Guide (Australia)

Chainsaw Man is one of the biggest anime and manga properties of the last few years, and its Weiss Schwarz set has been one of the more heavily traded modern releases on the platform since it launched. The series follows Denji, a young debt collector fused with his pet devil Pochita, who becomes the devil-human hybrid Chainsaw Man and is recruited into Tokyo Special Division 4 alongside Makima, Power, Aki Hayakawa, and the rest of the Public Safety cast.

## The set at a glance

Chainsaw Man is a single stand-alone set in English, released 16 February 2024, with a total of 207 cards. It has not received a second English wave, so this one release is currently the complete Chainsaw Man collection on the platform. The set code is CSM, and the release featured a total of 100 base card types plus 48 parallel versions, giving a genuinely broad rarity spread for a single-set release.

Boosters were packed 9 cards per pack, 16 packs per display, 18 displays per carton, and the release included the now-standard extras of an exclusive stitched playmat per carton and one of five exclusive PR cards per display box.

## Deck building around Denji and Division 4

The card pool is built around the Public Safety Saga cast, with Denji and his Chainsaw Man form as the mechanical centrepiece, supported by Makima, Power, Aki, Himeno, Kobeni, and Kishibe. A defining mechanic in the set lets certain Denji cards transform into his Chainsaw Man alter ego mid-game, which is one of the more thematically faithful mechanics Weiss Schwarz has built for a licensed property in recent years, directly mirroring the character's core transformation from the source material.

Because the whole cast comes from a single narrative arc rather than being split across multiple sets and story eras, deck building stays focused on one consistent power structure rather than requiring you to reconcile mechanics from different points in the story.

## Chase cards and known high-value pulls

Chainsaw Man has produced some of the most expensive single pulls of any recent Weiss Schwarz release. The signature rare version of Makima has been listed at prices well above most other cards in the current English catalogue, and a rare Chainsaw Man card himself has also commanded a significant premium on the secondary market. These are not typical price points for a Weiss Schwarz single, and they reflect just how strong demand for this property has been since launch.

Every pack also carries a chance of a hot-stamped signed card from the voice cast, including Toya Kikunosuke as both Denji and Chainsaw Man, Tomori Kusunoki as Makima, and Fairouz Ai as Power. These signed versions sit on top of the already strong base demand for the set's top rarities.

## Why Australian collectors are paying attention

Chainsaw Man's crossover appeal is unusually strong even by anime TCG standards. The manga and anime both broke into mainstream conversation well beyond the usual anime audience, and that reach has translated directly into card demand. Combined with a relatively recent release date, meaning most of the print run is still in circulation rather than scarce from age, this is a set where demand is currently driven by genuine ongoing popularity rather than nostalgia or scarcity.

For Australian buyers, that mix of strong demand and healthy supply is worth watching closely. Prices on the signature chase cards have shown they can move quickly when new merchandise or a new season of the anime brings fresh attention to the franchise, so this is one of the more actively priced sets in the current catalogue rather than one that sits quietly at a stable long-term value.

## Comparing Chainsaw Man to other recent shonen releases

Weiss Schwarz has released several major shonen properties in quick succession over the past few years, and Chainsaw Man stands out from that group for how quickly its top cards reached serious secondary market prices. Where most licensed anime sets settle into a stable pricing pattern within a few months of release, Chainsaw Man's signature Makima and top-rarity Chainsaw Man cards climbed rapidly and have stayed at a premium well after launch.

That pattern is worth understanding if you are deciding where to allocate a limited collecting budget across several current shonen sets. Chainsaw Man carries more upside if the franchise's popularity continues to grow, but it also carries more downside risk than a steadier, more established property, since prices this high can correct if demand cools. If you are collecting purely to play rather than to speculate on card values, the mid-tier commons and rares built around the wider Division 4 cast remain far more affordable and still build a fully functional deck.

## What to check before buying

1. Confirm whether a high-value listing is the standard rare, the signature rare, or a hot-stamped signed voice cast version, since these can differ substantially in price for what looks like the same card in a thumbnail image.
2. Cross-check the card number against the CSM set code, since Chainsaw Man's popularity means fakes and misattributed listings are more likely to turn up than with lower-demand sets.
3. Watch pricing trends rather than assuming a snapshot price is stable, given how actively this set has traded since release.
