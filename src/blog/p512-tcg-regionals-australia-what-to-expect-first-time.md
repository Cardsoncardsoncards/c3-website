A Regional Championship is different from a locals event in ways that are not always communicated clearly. The field is larger, the competition is more optimised, the environment is louder, and the mental load is higher across a full day or multi-day event. This guide explains what actually happens at an Australian TCG Regional so none of it surprises you on the day.

> **The most important mindset shift for your first Regional**
>
> Locals events have friendly competition. Regionals have prepared competition. Players you face at a Regional have tested their deck, know their matchups, and understand the meta. Coming in without that preparation is noticeable within the first few turns of game one. This guide helps you arrive ready, not surprised.

## What the Field Looks Like at an Australian Regional

Australian TCG Regionals typically draw 50 to 300+ players depending on the game and location. Yu-Gi-Oh Regionals and Pokemon Regionals in major cities can draw the larger numbers. Bandai game Regionals through TAK Games in 2026 vary from 40 to 150 players per event depending on the game's community size.

A field of 100 players usually runs 7 to 8 Swiss rounds. A field of 200+ may run 8 to 10 rounds over two days. The top 8 or top 16 advance to elimination play depending on field size and event rules.

The competitive variance at a 100+ player Regional is significant. You can lose two rounds and still make top cut with strong resistance.

You can go 7-1 and miss top cut if resistance is low. Understanding that the final standings include resistance tie-breakers helps you manage a strong record that does not advance.

## The Day Structure: What Happens When

Registration opens well before the event start. At large events, registration queues take 15 to 30 minutes.

Arriving early is not optional. Arriving exactly at start time means you may miss the player meeting and potentially round one.

The player meeting happens before round one. This is where judges explain rules and highlight format-specific clarifications.

Pay attention. Missing the player meeting is a poor start and can leave you unclear on the round structure.

Rounds run on a timer with a limit of 40 to 60 minutes for best-of-three formats. When time is called there are additional turns or a specific end-of-round procedure depending on the game.

Learn your game's time extension rules before attending. Handling end-of-round correctly is a competitive skill.

Between rounds there is usually 5 to 15 minutes before the next round is posted. Use this time to eat, drink water, visit the bathroom, and reset mentally. Fatigue is real at multi-round events.

## The Side Event Programme

Every Australian TCG Regional runs side events alongside the main event. Side events are one of the most underappreciated parts of attending a Regional.

For players who are eliminated from the main event, side events provide more competitive games, often in different formats, with separate prize structures. For players who make a poor start in Swiss, side events can make the day feel worthwhile regardless of main event performance.

Side events typically include single-elimination tournaments in both the main event format and alternate formats. Entry fees are separate from the main event. Prizes range from product packs to store credit depending on the organiser.

Do not leave a Regional early because you lost rounds in the main event. The side event programme usually has more games available.

## Card Trading at the Venue

Australian TCG Regional events have active card trading happening throughout the day. Individual players bring cards to trade or sell, and dealers often set up at larger events.

Card trading at venues creates opportunities to acquire specific cards you need at prices closer to market value than retail. It also creates risk if you do not know current AUD market rates for cards you are selling.

Before attending any Regional where you plan to trade or sell cards, check the [C3 Card Vault](/cards) for current AUD prices on the cards you own and the cards you want. Walking into a trading environment without price knowledge puts you at a disadvantage.

## Handling a Losing Record at Your First Regional

Most players lose more rounds than they win at their first Regional. This is normal.

The average player at a 100-player Regional goes approximately 4-4 in Swiss. The top 8 go 7-1 or better. The players at the top have typically attended 5 to 20 Regionals before the one you are attending together.

Losing rounds at a Regional is information. Pay attention to which game states you lost in and why.

Were you losing to a specific strategy you were not prepared for? Were you misplaying your own deck? Were you facing a deck you had never practised against?

The most valuable thing you can do after losing a round at your first Regional is ask your opponent what they would have done differently in your position. Most competitive players at Australian events are willing to discuss game states after a match.

## The Multi-Day Regional Experience

Some Australian Regionals run across two or three days. The Brisbane DBS and Gundam Regionals, the Yu-Gi-Oh Oceanic WCQ, and the Magic Spotlight: The Hobbit all run across multiple days.

Multi-day events require planning for accommodation, food, and physical recovery. Playing competitive TCG for 8 to 10 hours on consecutive days is more taxing than it sounds. Eating properly between rounds, sleeping enough, and staying hydrated are not minor considerations at a three-day event.

If you are travelling interstate for a multi-day Regional, build your schedule with the round counts in mind. Events that start at 9am and run until 8pm or later leave little time for anything else. Plan for the event to fill your days completely.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How many rounds does an Australian TCG Regional typically run?**
Depends on field size. 50 to 100 players usually runs 6 to 7 Swiss rounds. 100 to 200 players runs 7 to 8 rounds. 200+ players may run 9 to 10 rounds. Multi-day Regionals like the Yu-Gi-Oh WCQ split Swiss rounds across two days.

**What happens if I arrive late to a Regional?**
Late registration policies vary by game and organiser. Some events allow late registration within a specific window with a round one bye. Others do not. Arriving before registration closes is always the safe approach.

**Can I drop from a Regional partway through?**
Yes, but you should notify the event team before dropping so your opponents are not waiting for a match against a player who has already left. Dropping early and joining side events is a legitimate choice if your main event is not going well.

**What is the difference between Swiss standings and top cut?**
Swiss is the main event rounds where everyone plays every round. Standings determine which players advance to the top cut. Top cut is single-elimination, meaning one loss eliminates you from the tournament.
