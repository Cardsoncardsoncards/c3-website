# Rurouni Kenshin Weiss Schwarz Card Guide (Australia)

Rurouni Kenshin follows Himura Kenshin, a former assassin known as Hitokiri Battosai during the Bakumatsu period, who renounces killing and becomes a wandering swordsman protecting the people of Meiji era Japan. Based on the manga that has sold over 72 million copies, the franchise is one of the true classics of the samurai and Meiji era genre, and its Weiss Schwarz release brings that legacy directly onto the card table.

## The set at a glance

Rurouni Kenshin is a single stand-alone set in English, released 30 May 2025, with a total of 268 cards, based on the newest anime adaptation covering the first season of the story. The set code is RKN, and the release featured 100 base card types plus 108 parallel versions, packed 8 cards per pack, 12 packs per display, 24 displays per carton.

The Booster Pack features 10 newly drawn illustrations across the core cast, including Kenshin Himura, Kaoru Kamiya, Yahiko Myojin, Sanosuke Sagara, Megumi Takagi, Aoshi Shinomori, Hajime Saito, and Tsubame Sanjo, giving the release genuine breadth across the story's memorable Meiji era ensemble.

## Deck building around the Meiji Romantic Story cast

The card pool is built around the shared trait Meiji Romantic Story, with numerous cards checking for the presence of other characters carrying that same trait to boost their own power. Kenshin himself, in his Hitokiri Battosai persona, provides some of the set's most powerful resonance mechanics, requiring you to reveal multiple copies of his card from hand to trigger devastating attacks, a genuinely faithful nod to his history as the feared assassin beneath his peaceful exterior.

Supporting characters like Kaoru Kamiya, the spirited dojo instructor, and Aoshi Shinomori, leader of the Oniwabanshu, round out the deck building options, giving players a real choice between building a tight Kenshin focused deck or a broader ensemble built around the wider Meiji Romantic Story trait.

## Chase cards and signature pulls

Every pack carries a chance of a foiled SP or rarer SEC secret card, with foil stamped signatures from the voice cast, including three variations signed by Soma Saito as Kenshin Himura and a signed Kaoru Kamiya card from Rie Takahashi. Display boxes included one of eight Going First and Going Second cards as a box topper, and first edition cartons carried an English Edition exclusive stitched playmat.

It is worth noting that the artwork for the SP and SEC cards was modified from the original Japanese edition, so English and Japanese versions of the same numbered card can differ visually, a detail worth checking if comparing listings across both markets.

## Why this property carries lasting collector appeal

Rurouni Kenshin is a genuine classic of the samurai anime and manga genre, with a manga sales record that places it among the best selling series of all time. This latest anime adaptation reintroduced the story to a new generation of viewers while still carrying deep nostalgic weight for longtime fans, giving the card set a rare combination of fresh contemporary demand and established franchise prestige.

For Australian collectors, that dual appeal makes Rurouni Kenshin one of the more promising recent releases in the current one-set tier, with a manga readership in the tens of millions providing a genuinely large potential collector base well beyond the current anime's immediate viewership. That combination of scale and freshness is relatively rare among current Weiss Schwarz releases.

## A classic manga finding new relevance

Rurouni Kenshin originally ran in the 1990s, well before most currently active Weiss Schwarz collectors would have been introduced to anime as a hobby, and this newest adaptation represents a genuine attempt to bring that classic story to a contemporary audience rather than simply serving existing fans. That positioning is worth understanding when thinking about this set's likely trajectory, since it is trying to build a new fanbase in real time rather than resting purely on established nostalgia.

For collectors, this dual audience, longtime manga readers rediscovering the franchise alongside entirely new viewers encountering it for the first time through this adaptation, creates a broader potential demand base than a purely nostalgia driven re-release would typically produce. It is a genuinely interesting case study in how a decades old classic can find fresh relevance when given a well produced modern adaptation.

## What to check before buying

1. Confirm whether a listing is base rarity, an OFR overframe rare, or a signed SP or SEC version before paying a premium, given the wide 108 card parallel spread across this release.
2. If comparing English and Japanese listings for the same card number, check the artwork carefully, since SP and SEC card illustrations were modified between the two editions.
3. Decide whether you are building a tight Kenshin focused resonance deck or a broader Meiji Romantic Story ensemble before buying broadly, since the two approaches draw on different parts of the card pool.
