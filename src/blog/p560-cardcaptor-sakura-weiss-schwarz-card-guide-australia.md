# Cardcaptor Sakura Weiss Schwarz Card Guide (Australia)

Cardcaptor Sakura is one of the genuine classics of the shoujo magical girl genre, and its Weiss Schwarz set draws specifically on the Clear Card arc, the continuation series that brought Sakura Kinomoto and her friends back for a new generation of fans while still carrying decades of nostalgia for longtime readers of the original manga and anime.

## The set at a glance

Cardcaptor Sakura: Clear Card is a single stand-alone set in the current English Weiss Schwarz catalogue, released 22 February 2019, with a total of 170 cards. It has not received a second English wave since, so everything currently available for this property in English sits in this one release. Note that a newer Cardcaptor Sakura 25th Anniversary set has appeared in Japan, but as it has not yet been added to our tracked card data, this guide covers the confirmed Clear Card set only. We will update this page if and when the newer set is added to our database.

The set followed the standard structure of a Trial Deck alongside the main Booster Pack, plus a later Supply Set bundling a deck box, sleeves, and boosters together, making it accessible to newer players who wanted to build around Sakura without hunting down singles individually.

## Building around Sakura and the Clear Cards

The Clear Card arc reimagines Sakura's original set of magical Clow Cards as blank Clear Cards that she must recapture and redefine, giving the set's card pool a strong central hook around Sakura herself, supported by Syaoran Li, Kero, and the wider cast of friends and family that has anchored the franchise since its original run. Because the set draws from a single arc rather than the sprawling original series, the card pool stays tightly focused rather than trying to cover every era of the franchise at once.

This makes deck building here more straightforward than properties with a scattered, multi-era card pool. You are working from one coherent narrative arc, which keeps the available synergies focused around a consistent cast rather than forcing you to pick and choose across decades of source material.

## Why this property carries long-term collector interest

Cardcaptor Sakura carries a rare combination for a Weiss Schwarz property: genuine nostalgia value from an anime that predates the TCG itself by well over a decade, combined with a modern arc that introduced the series to new viewers who had no prior history with the franchise. That combination has kept demand steady in a way that purely nostalgia-driven or purely current-hype properties do not always manage on their own.

For Australian collectors, this makes Cardcaptor Sakura a reasonably safe long-term hold within the anime TCG space. Its fanbase is established and has been buying merchandise and collectibles for the franchise for decades, which tends to produce steadier, less speculative pricing than newer, hype-driven releases.

## A note on the newer 25th Anniversary release

Bushiroad marked Cardcaptor Sakura's 25th anniversary with a fresh Japanese-language set, released alongside recent hits like Project SEKAI COLORFUL STAGE! and other current-generation properties, which tells you the franchise is still considered a strong enough seller to warrant a brand new release decades after the original manga launched. That is a genuinely unusual position for a franchise this old to be in, and it says something about how durable the Cardcaptor Sakura fanbase has proven to be.

At the time of writing, this newer set has not yet made its way into our tracked card database, so it sits outside the scope of this guide. If you come across listings for the 25th Anniversary set (card code CCS/W113) while shopping, be aware that it is a separate release from the Clear Card set covered here, with its own distinct card pool, pricing, and rarity structure. We will publish a dedicated guide for that set once it is fully synced into our data.

## What to check before buying

1. Confirm you are buying from the Clear Card set (CCS/W66) and not accidentally from the newer 25th Anniversary Japanese release, since the two share character names and could be confused in a quick listing scan.
2. Higher rarity Sakura and Syaoran cards carry a genuine premium due to sustained collector demand, so compare several listings before buying rather than taking the first price you see.
3. Check whether a listing includes parallel or foil variants, since the Clear Card set includes 26 parallel versions on top of the 100 base card types, and pricing between the two can differ significantly.

## Collecting for nostalgia versus collecting for play

Cardcaptor Sakura is a good example of a set where a meaningful share of buyers are not deck builders at all. A large portion of demand for this property comes from people who grew up with the original series and simply want key art of Sakura, Syaoran, and Kero on physical cards, regardless of how those cards perform in a competitive deck. If that describes your own interest in this set, it is worth focusing your budget on the cards featuring your favourite scenes and characters rather than chasing whatever the current competitive tier list suggests is strongest, since resale value here tends to track character popularity and artwork quality as much as in-game power level.
