Pokemon Draft is one of the most fun ways to play Pokemon TCG with a group and doesn't get nearly the attention it deserves. No existing decks required. Everyone starts from the same card pool. The skills are card evaluation and deck building under constraints — not who has the better collection.

This guide covers how to run a Pokemon draft at home in Australia.

## What Pokemon Draft Is

Pokemon Draft works similarly to MTG Draft: players open packs, take one card, pass the rest, and build a deck from the cards they draft.

After drafting, each player builds a 40-card deck from their drafted cards plus basic Energy cards (provided separately). Players then play matches against each other.

The appeal: no existing collection advantage. Everyone starts equal. The skill is in evaluating which cards are good in a limited card pool and building a coherent deck from whatever you drafted.

## What You Need

**Players**: 4 to 8 players works best. Pokemon Draft with 4 is fun; 6 to 8 is better for pack diversity.

**Booster packs**: 3 packs per player. For 4 players: 12 packs. For 6 players: 18 packs.

**Basic Energy**: you don't draft Energy cards — instead, have a shared energy station with a pile of each basic Energy type. Players pick as many basic Energy cards as they need to complete their 40-card deck.

**Cost**: Pokemon booster packs cost approximately AU$7 to AU$9 each. 3 packs per player = approximately AU$21 to AU$27 per person. Players keep their drafted cards.

## The Draft Rules

**Round 1**: each player opens their first pack, reviews all cards, selects one card to keep, then passes the remaining pack to the player on their left. Continue picking and passing until all cards are taken.

**Round 2**: open second pack, pass to the right this time.

**Round 3**: open third pack, pass to the left again (alternating direction each round).

After three rounds, each player has 45 drafted cards (15 per pack, one taken per pick pass, 3 packs) plus unlimited access to basic Energy.

## Building Your Pokemon Draft Deck

From your 45 drafted cards, build a 40-card deck:

- Minimum 40 cards total
- You choose the Energy type(s) based on what Pokemon you drafted
- No limit on Energy cards (draw from the communal pile)
- All 45 cards are in play — choose your best 40 and exclude the others (your sideboard)

**The key decision**: which 1 or 2 Energy types to run. Most Pokemon in a limited card pool require 1 or 2 types of Energy for their attacks. Focusing on 1 type gives better consistency.

Aim for approximately 14 to 16 Energy, 12 to 14 Pokemon, and 10 to 12 Trainer cards.

## Deck Building Tips for Pokemon Draft

**Prioritise Pokemon with low Energy requirements**: in draft, you rarely have perfect Energy consistency. Pokemon that attack for 1 or 2 Energy of a single type are significantly more reliable than those needing 3 or requiring two different types.

**Take draw Trainer cards highly**: Supporter cards that let you draw cards (Professor's Research, Marnie equivalents) are more valuable in draft than in constructed because your card pool is more inconsistent and you need to see more of your deck.

**Don't force a weak type**: if you've drafted 3 Fire Pokemon and 8 Water Pokemon, build around Water even if the Fire Pokemon are individually stronger.

## Match Structure

Play 1v1 matches using the full Pokemon TCG rules. For casual home draft nights, best-of-1 matches work well for time efficiency. For a more competitive structure, best-of-3 with a sideboard (your unchosen 5 cards) is standard.

**Prize cards**: use the standard 6 Prize Card rule.

## Set Selection for Pokemon Draft

Some Pokemon sets draft better than others. Sets with:

- Consistent Energy requirements within each type
- A reasonable mix of Basic Pokemon (you need these to start)
- Trainer cards that work in any deck (not requiring specific types)

...tend to produce better draft experiences. Recent Scarlet and Violet sets are generally well-designed for limited play. Very old sets can have inconsistent limited environments.
