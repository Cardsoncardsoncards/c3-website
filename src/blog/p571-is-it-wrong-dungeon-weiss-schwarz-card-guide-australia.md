# Is It Wrong to Try to Pick Up Girls in a Dungeon Weiss Schwarz Card Guide (Australia)

Is It Wrong to Try to Pick Up Girls in a Dungeon, often shortened to DanMachi by fans, follows Bell Cranel, a young adventurer guided by his goddess Hestia, as he ventures into the Dungeon seeking money, fame, glory, and the attention of the many capable women who populate the story around him. It is one of the longer running light novel and anime franchises to reach Weiss Schwarz, and its set brings together a genuinely large cast of adventurers, goddesses, and Dungeon dwellers.

## The set at a glance

Is It Wrong to Try to Pick Up Girls in a Dungeon is a single stand-alone set in English, released 15 July 2022, with a total of 176 cards. It has not received a second English wave, so this remains the full available collection for this property. The set code is DDM, and the release featured 100 base card types plus 35 parallel versions, packed 9 cards per pack, 16 packs per display, 18 displays per carton.

The card pool covers Bell Cranel and Hestia at its core, alongside a wide supporting cast including Ais Wallenstein, Liliruca Arde, Eina Tulle, Syr Flova, and Ryu Lion, giving genuine depth across the franchise's large ensemble of female characters rather than concentrating entirely on the two central figures.

## Deck building around Bell and the Hestia Familia

Because the source material leans heavily on Bell's relationships with the many strong women around him, the card pool is built to let you construct decks around specific character groupings rather than forcing a single dominant strategy. Ais Wallenstein, one of the strongest adventurers in the setting, tends to anchor more aggressive, high power builds, while Hestia and the Hestia Familia cards support a steadier, growth focused playstyle that mirrors Bell's own development through the story.

This spread of distinct characters with genuinely different mechanical identities gives the set solid build variety, and it means new players can choose to build around whichever character from the show's large cast appeals to them most, rather than being funnelled toward one obvious best option.

## Chase cards and signature pulls

Every pack carries a chance of a signed card from the voice cast, including Yoshitsugu Matsuoka as Bell Cranel, Inori Minase as Hestia, Saori Oonishi as Ais Wallenstein, and further signatures across Liliruca Arde, Eina Tulle, Syr Flova, and Ryu Lion. Display boxes included one of four exclusive PR cards, and first edition cartons carried an English Edition exclusive stitched playmat.

Given the size of the cast covered by this release, signature chase cards are spread across seven different characters rather than concentrated on just one or two leads, which gives collectors a genuinely broad set of options depending on which character they want to chase specifically.

## Why this property continues to draw collectors

DanMachi has one of the longer running light novel adaptations to reach Weiss Schwarz, having produced multiple anime seasons and spin off material well beyond most single season licensed properties. That longevity has built a substantial, ongoing fanbase that continues to follow new instalments of the story, which has helped sustain demand for the card set well past its initial release window.

For Australian collectors, the wide cast covered by this set is a genuine strength. Rather than betting on a single character's popularity holding up over time, a DanMachi collection spreads interest across a large ensemble, which tends to produce steadier overall demand than a set built around just one or two central figures.

## Collecting an ensemble cast set versus a two character set

DanMachi is a good example of the trade offs involved in collecting a set built around a large ensemble rather than a tightly focused pair or trio of leads. On one hand, spreading demand across seven or more distinct characters means no single card carries the outsized premium you sometimes see in tightly focused sets where all collector attention concentrates on one or two figures. On the other hand, building a truly complete collection of every character you care about can require tracking down cards across a wider spread of the print run than a more concentrated set would.

For a new collector deciding where to start, this makes DanMachi a reasonable pick if your interest is broad, spanning several characters from the show rather than one specific favourite. If you are chasing a single character intensely, a more tightly focused set elsewhere in the catalogue might get you to a complete collection of that one character's available cards more quickly and at lower overall cost.
