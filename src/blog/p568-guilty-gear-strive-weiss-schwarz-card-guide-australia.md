# Guilty Gear Strive Weiss Schwarz Card Guide (Australia)

Guilty Gear -Strive- is one of the best selling titles in the fighting game genre, and its Weiss Schwarz release marked one of the relatively rare occasions the platform has adapted a video game rather than an anime or manga property. For fighting game fans who also collect TCGs, this is one of the more interesting crossovers on the platform.

## The set at a glance

Guilty Gear -Strive- is a single stand-alone set, released as an English Edition Original on 15 December 2023, with a total of 216 cards. Being an English Edition Original means the set was created specifically for the English market, with no earlier Japanese print run to compare pricing against. The set code is GGST, and the release featured 100 base card types plus 56 parallel versions, packed 9 cards per pack, 16 packs per display, 18 displays per carton.

The card pool covers the game's main roster of Fighters, with newly created card art rather than reused in game character art, giving the set a distinct visual identity separate from the video game itself.

## Deck building around the Guilty Gear roster

Because Guilty Gear -Strive- is a fighting game rather than a story driven anime, the card pool is organised around its roster of playable Fighters, including Nagoriyuki and May among the more prominent names featured. Each Fighter typically carries their own distinct playstyle translated into card mechanics, giving genuine build variety across the set rather than concentrating power around one or two central characters the way some narrative driven properties do.

This structure suits players who already know the game and have a favourite Fighter, since building a Weiss Schwarz deck around that same character carries a natural appeal beyond just the mechanics.

## Chase cards and signature pulls

This set includes some genuinely unusual chase items compared to a typical anime licensed release. Alongside standard SP special rare cards with gold hot stamping, the set features frameless OFR, Over Frame Rare, cards with illustrations specially drawn by artist hncl. Most notably, the set includes a SEC secret rare card signed by Daisuke Ishiwatari, the actual developer and creator of Guilty Gear. A developer signature is a genuinely rare kind of chase card on this platform, well outside the usual voice cast autograph format, and it reflects the unique position of Guilty Gear as a game rather than a licensed anime adaptation.

Display boxes included one of ten exclusive PR cards, and first edition cartons carried four rubber playmats, giving collectors a broad spread of accessory chase items on top of the core card pool.

## Why fighting game fans are buying into this set

Guilty Gear has built one of the more dedicated fanbases in the fighting game genre, known for its distinctive rock inspired soundtrack, deep competitive mechanics, and strong character design. That fanbase brings a genuinely different kind of collector to this set compared to most Weiss Schwarz properties, one drawn from competitive gaming culture rather than anime fandom specifically.

For Australian collectors, the developer signed SEC card is the standout item to be aware of in this set. Cards signed by an actual creator rather than a voice actor carry a different kind of collector prestige, and demand for that specific card has reflected its rarity within the release. It is arguably the single most distinctive chase card across the entire one-set tier of properties covered in this guide series.

## Video game adaptations versus anime adaptations on Weiss Schwarz

Guilty Gear -Strive- is a useful case study in how Weiss Schwarz handles video game properties differently from its usual anime and manga source material. Because there is no ongoing anime or manga narrative to draw new card art and storylines from, video game adaptations on this platform tend to be single, self contained releases rather than properties that receive regular follow up sets tied to new seasons or story arcs. That makes collecting complete for a set like this genuinely achievable in a way that an ongoing anime property with the possibility of future waves is not.

It also means demand for this set is driven by the game's ongoing competitive and cultural relevance rather than seasonal anime hype cycles. As long as Guilty Gear -Strive- remains an actively played and followed fighting game, interest in the card set should track that community's size and engagement rather than fluctuating with a broadcast schedule the way anime tie in sets often do.

## What to check before buying

1. Confirm whether a listing is the standard SP hot stamped version, the OFR frameless illustration, or the developer signed SEC card, since these carry very different value despite superficially similar descriptions.
2. Check which Fighter a card represents before buying if you are building around a specific character from the game, since the roster spans multiple playstyles rather than a single dominant archetype.
3. Because this is an English Edition Original with no Japanese comparison market, rely on English market listings specifically to judge fair pricing rather than assuming a Japanese equivalent exists to check against.
