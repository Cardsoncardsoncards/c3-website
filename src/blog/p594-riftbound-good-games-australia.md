Riftbound, Riot Games' League of Legends trading card game, has quickly become one of the most
talked-about games on the Australian tabletop scene, and Good Games has kept pace with it, running
weekly "Nexus Night" constructed events across multiple stores. It's also one of five games
featured in Good Games' 2026 Championship Series, with a share of the combined $3,000 prize pool
on the line, a fast turnaround for a game that's barely a year into its life.

## An unusually aggressive release schedule

Riftbound launched in China in August 2025, with the English release following in October 2025.
Since then, Riot has kept an aggressive cadence going, Set 2 "Spiritforged" landed in February
2026, and Set 3 "Unleashed" followed in May, introducing Master Yi, Diana, Vi, Pyke and Kha'Zix as
Champion Legends, alongside new mechanics and a new "Ultimate" rarity tier sitting above standard
Champion Legends in scarcity.

Set 4, "Vendetta," is the one to watch right now, releasing 31 July 2026. It's notable for being
Riftbound's first truly global simultaneous release, no regional head start anywhere, meaning
Australian players get access to new cards at exactly the same moment as everyone else worldwide
for the first time. Reveal season for Vendetta began 22 June 2026, and the set is confirmed to
introduce nine Champion Legends, including Nasus, Renekton, Akali, Mel, Ambessa, Zed and Shen.

Vendetta also debuts Riftbound's first two-player starter product, Showdown Decks, a Zed versus
Shen matchup built specifically for jumping straight into games with a friend rather than needing
to build a full competitive deck first. Set 5, "Radiance," is already confirmed for Q4 2026.

## Why the pace matters

Three major sets in a single year, with a fourth already locked in for later in the year, is an
unusually fast cadence for a TCG, especially one still establishing itself. It signals real
confidence from Riot in the game's momentum, and it also means the competitive metagame is moving
quickly, what's dominant in the Unleashed era won't necessarily carry over once Vendetta's nine
new Champion Legends land at the end of July.

For a card game built on League of Legends, one of the biggest gaming IPs in the world, that speed
also makes sense as a strategy. Riftbound isn't trying to slowly build an audience from scratch,
it's converting an already-massive existing fanbase, and a fast release cadence keeps that
audience engaged in a way that mirrors how League of Legends itself has always operated as a
live-service game with a constant stream of new content.

## What this means if you're playing in the Championship

With Vendetta landing at the very end of July, right in the thick of the Championship Series
window, players who've been focused on the Unleashed meta should expect the competitive format
to shift meaningfully once the new Champion Legends are available. If you're building toward a
Championship or Nexus Night event now, decide whether you're optimising for the current meta or
already planning around what Vendetta brings, since a deck built purely around Unleashed staples

## Belconnen's Monday Nexus Nights

Good Games Belconnen runs one of the more established weekly Nexus Night events, giving Canberra-
area players a consistent, dated event to build toward rather than relying on one-off tournaments.
Regular, predictable events like this tend to matter more for a fast-moving game like Riftbound
than for a slower, more established TCG, since players need somewhere reliable to actually test a
new deck against real opponents as the meta shifts with each set.

## Getting started

If you haven't tried Riftbound yet, Good Games' weekly Nexus Nights are the natural entry point,
and Vendetta's new Showdown Decks, built specifically as a two-player starter product, look like a
genuinely accessible way to jump in with a friend rather than needing to build a full competitive
deck from scratch first.

## A different kind of collector market

Because Riftbound draws directly on League of Legends characters rather than an original card-game
setting, its collector base includes a fair number of people buying primarily for the IP rather
than to compete, League fans picking up cards featuring their favourite champions regardless of
whether those cards are strong in the current format. That's a similar dynamic to how licensed
crossover sets in other TCGs tend to behave, and it's part of why Champion Legend pricing can move
independently of raw competitive strength, popularity of the underlying character matters too.

## Looking ahead

Between Vendetta's global simultaneous release landing at the end of this month and Radiance
already locked in for Q4, Riftbound shows no sign of slowing its pace through the rest of 2026.
Watch closely how the new Champion Legends reshape the competitive scene once Vendetta actually
lands, that's usually where real price movement follows, not at the announcement stage.
