Not all Australian TCG buylists pay the same. Stores set their buying prices independently based on their current inventory, their customer demand, and their own pricing strategy. The practical result is that the same card in the same condition can have a buylist price that varies by 30 to 50 percent between different Australian stores on the same day.

This guide covers the major Australian stores with active buylists, what drives price differences between them, and how to get the best return on your cards.

## The Australian TCG Buylist Landscape

Several Australian stores maintain active, searchable online buylists. The main ones are:

**Good Games** operates the largest national TCG store network in Australia and runs a searchable online buylist via BinderPOS. They buy MTG, Pokemon, and One Piece singles, with in-store processing at any Good Games location or mail-in to their Parramatta NSW office. Their national presence makes them the most accessible option for most Australian sellers.

**TCG Singles** (Lismore NSW) buys MTG, Pokemon, and other games via an online buylist tool. Mail-in accepted from anywhere in Australia. They charge a 15 percent processing fee on submissions that arrive unsorted or still sleeved, so read their submission requirements before sending.

**Ronin Games** (ACT) buys MTG and Pokemon singles, with mail-in accepted from interstate. They publish clear bulk rates alongside their singles buylist, which makes them straightforward for sellers with a mix of high-value and bulk cards.

**Gameology** (VIC, two locations) buys Pokemon, One Piece, and Lorcana singles. Not a comprehensive MTG buylist. Better suited to Pokemon and modern game sellers in Victoria.

**Mana Market** (Sydney area) uses an eBay consignment model rather than a traditional buylist, handling the listing and sale process on your behalf for a fee. This can return more than a standard buylist for high-value singles but takes longer and suits different situations.

## What Drives Price Differences Between Stores?

Understanding why prices differ helps you predict which store is likely to offer more for your specific cards.

**Current inventory.** A store that already has 20 copies of a card in stock has little reason to offer a high buylist price for more. A store that has sold out and has customer demand for the card will price its buylist higher to attract sellers. You cannot know this without checking each store's current price.

**Store focus and customer base.** A store with a strong competitive MTG player base needs current Standard and Pioneer staples urgently. A store focused on casual and collector product has different demand. Their buylists reflect this.

**Geographic demand.** A Melbourne-based store serves Melbourne players. A card heavily played in the Melbourne meta may have higher buylist demand at a VIC-based store than at a store in another state.

**Cash vs store credit economics.** Every store sets its own spread between cash and credit rates. Some stores are more generous with store credit than others, which affects which store is best for you depending on your payment preference.

## The Price Variation Is Real and Significant

This is not a minor difference. On any given card worth AU$20 to AU$50, the spread between the highest and lowest Australian buylist price is commonly AU$3 to AU$8. On higher-value cards, the spread widens further.

A collection of 50 cards with an average buylist value of AU$10 per card, sold to the highest-paying store for each card rather than to a single store, could return AU$50 to AU$150 more than just accepting the first offer. This is a real, achievable difference that requires only the time to check multiple buylists before submitting.

The problem is that checking multiple Australian buylists individually is time-consuming. Each store uses a different tool, a different search interface, and a different price format. There is currently no single place to compare Australian buylist prices across stores simultaneously — which is exactly the gap a buylist aggregator fills.

## Store Credit vs Cash: Know the Difference Before You Submit

Every Australian buylist offers two rates. Store credit is always higher. The typical gap is:

- Cash rate: 40 to 60 percent of market value
- Store credit rate: 60 to 80 percent of market value

If you buy regularly from a store and can use their credit effectively, the store credit rate is almost always the better financial choice. If you want cash in your bank account, accept the cash rate — but know that switching to store credit would put more value in your hands for the same cards.

## Practical Approach: How to Get the Best Buylist Price in Australia

**Step 1.** Sort your cards by approximate value. Anything worth AU$5 or more per card is worth checking individually across multiple stores.

**Step 2.** Check at least two buylists for your highest-value cards. Good Games and TCG Singles are a solid starting pair for MTG. For Pokemon, Good Games and Gameology cover most of the market.

**Step 3.** Look at which store is paying more for your specific cards and note whether the difference is enough to justify splitting your submission or changing your target store.

**Step 4.** Decide on cash versus store credit before submitting. If you plan to use the credit, choose the store you buy from most regularly — the higher credit rate plus the convenience of spending at a familiar store usually makes this a clear choice.

**Step 5.** Submit to the store offering the best overall return for your specific card mix, not just the store with the highest name recognition.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Which Australian store pays the most for MTG cards?**
It varies by card. No single store consistently pays the most across all cards. The store with the highest demand for a specific card will offer the most for it. The only way to know is to check multiple stores for your specific cards.

**How do I find Australian TCG buylists?**
Good Games, TCG Singles, Ronin Games, and Gameology all have searchable online buylists. Search each store's website for their buylist or buy list page.

**Is store credit or cash better on a TCG buylist?**
Store credit is always the higher rate, typically 20 to 30 percent more than the cash equivalent. Cash is better if you need money or do not buy regularly from that store.

**How often do Australian buylist prices change?**
Frequently. Prices update based on market movements, store inventory levels, and demand shifts. A price you checked a week ago may be different today. Always check current prices immediately before submitting.

**Do Australian stores buy Pokemon and Lorcana on buylists too?**
Yes. Good Games buys Pokemon and One Piece. Gameology buys Pokemon, One Piece, and Lorcana. TCG Singles buys Pokemon. Coverage varies by game and by store.

**What is the minimum card value worth submitting on a buylist?**
Most Australian stores set a minimum buylist value per card of AU$0.50 to AU$1.00 for individual singles. Below that threshold, cards are treated as bulk and bought by weight or lot rather than individually.
