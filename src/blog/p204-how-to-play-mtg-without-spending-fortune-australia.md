MTG has an expensive reputation. At the high end, competitive Modern and Legacy decks cost thousands. But casual MTG — where most players actually play — has genuinely accessible price points if you choose your format wisely.

This guide covers every way to play MTG in Australia without a significant spend.

## The Expensive Formats (Avoid if Budget Is the Priority)

**Modern**: needs specific high-value cards that cost AU$30 to AU$100+ each. A competitive Modern deck in Australia costs AU$500 to AU$2,000+.

**Legacy**: the most expensive format. Dual lands and Power Nine cards. Not practical for casual players.

**Standard**: theoretically cheaper (only uses current sets), but building competitively requires chasing the current meta cards, which fluctuates with every new release.

## The Budget-Friendly Formats

### Commander with Budget Decks

Commander is the most popular format in Australia and has the most accessible budget entry point.

The key insight: Commander doesn't require cards that compete with other Commanders. A AU$100 Commander deck wins games. It won't beat a AU$1,000 Commander deck reliably, but at a casual table of similar-budget players, it's completely functional.

**How to start**: buy one preconstructed Commander deck (AU$60 to AU$80). Play it for a month. Upgrade with singles from the [C3 eBay store](https://www.ebay.com.au/str/cardsoncardsoncards) targeting the weakest parts of the deck first.

**Budget ceiling for a fun experience**: AU$100 to AU$150. Above that, you're optimising rather than enjoying.

See our [budget Commander deck building guide](/blog/budget-commander-deck-under-100-australia/).

### Pauper

Pauper is a format where only common-rarity cards are legal. Commons cost AU$0.20 to AU$2 each. A full Pauper deck costs AU$20 to AU$80 depending on the specific list.

Pauper has a surprisingly deep strategic environment because many powerful cards have appeared at common rarity over 30 years of Magic.

**The challenge**: Pauper has a smaller Australian player base than Commander. Finding opponents locally may require online play (Magic: The Gathering Arena has a Pauper mode).

### Limited: Draft and Sealed

In Limited formats (Draft and Sealed), you build your deck from cards you open on the day. No collection required. No pre-built decks.

**Sealed**: pay an entry fee (typically AU$25 to AU$40), open 6 booster packs, build a 40-card deck, play.

**Draft**: pay an entry fee (typically AU$25 to AU$40 depending on the store), draft 3 packs with 7 other players, build a 40-card deck, play.

You keep the cards you open. For players who enjoy the game but don't want to invest in a collection, Limited is the most sustainable format — your "investment" is the entry fee and you leave with cards regardless.

### Magic Arena (Digital, Free to Start)

MTG Arena is Wizards of the Coast's free-to-play digital client. You can play Standard, Alchemy, Draft, and Pauper formats without spending real money (with slower progression) or with targeted spending on gems for Draft entries.

Not physical cards, but if cost is the primary concern and you want to play MTG competitively, Arena removes the expensive card acquisition requirement.

### Kitchen Table MTG with Starter Products

For two players playing casually at home: the MTG Starter Kit (AU$22, two complete decks) is everything you need. You can play hundreds of games with the same two decks without it feeling repetitive.

Add a second Starter Kit (different theme decks) and you have four distinct deck matchups for AU$44.

## The Budget Rule: Know Your Power Level Table

The most important factor in enjoying budget MTG is playing with other players at similar budgets. A AU$100 Commander deck at a table of AU$100 Commander decks is a great experience. The same deck at a table of AU$500 decks is not fun.

Talk to your local game store about their Commander nights and the typical power level. Most stores have casual nights specifically for lower-power play.
