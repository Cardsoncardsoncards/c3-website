Yu-Gi-Oh's road to the World Championship in Tokyo hit its biggest week of the year between 11 and
19 July 2026, and Australia was right in the middle of it. The Oceanic World Championship Qualifier
was held at Malvern Town Hall in Melbourne on 11 and 12 July, the same weekend North America,
Europe and Korea ran their own qualifiers. The Yu-Gi-Oh World Championship 2026 itself runs 29 to
30 August at the Tokyo Garden Theater in Ariake.

## Melbourne sends its Duelists to Tokyo

The 2026 Oceanic WCQ drew 299 players to Malvern Town Hall, and Jerry Zhang came out on top with a
Fiendsmith deck built around [Fiendsmith Engraver](/cards/yugioh/the-infinite-forbidden-fiendsmith-engraver-quarter-century-secret-rare-info-en017).
Dillon Taylor finished runner-up on Power Patron Artmage, Frankie Lin took third with Branded
Despia, and Stephen Medhurst rounded out the Top 4 on Kewl Tune. Zhang and Taylor, as the two
finalists, both earned direct invitations to the World Championship in Tokyo.

## Why Oceania's path to Worlds is different

Oceania is the only Yu-Gi-Oh region that never adopted the World Qualifying Points system that
North America, Europe and the Americas use. Instead, the only way an Oceanic Duelist reaches the
World Championship is by making the final of the Oceanic Championship itself. That makes the
Melbourne result higher stakes than a typical Regional, since anything short of the final means
waiting another year for the next shot at Tokyo.

## North America: Ryan Yu goes undefeated

The North America World Championship Qualifier ran the same weekend in Minneapolis, Minnesota,
with 2,077 Duelists competing across 18 rounds. Ryan Yu of Ontario, Canada, won every single round
with a [Sky Striker](/cards/yugioh/battles-of-legend-monstrous-revenge-sky-striker-mobilize-engage-quarter-century-secret-rare-blmr-en091)
deck to take the title without dropping a match. Yu is a former Dragon Duel World Champion and
former Master Duel World Champion, and he beat friend and fellow competitor Charley Futch, running
Kewl Tune, in the final.

## Europe and Korea crown their own fields

The European WCQ also ran that weekend in Brussels, Belgium, drawing 2,205 players, with the top
four earning direct invitations to Tokyo. Korea's WCQ was smaller and invite only, held in Seoul on
11 July with 141 participants drawn from the country's Regional Qualifiers and Shop Qualifiers.
Both events feed the same World Championship field that North America and Oceania's qualifiers do,
just through different regional pathways.

## Japan Championship wraps the week

Japan ran its own invite-only Championship on 18 and 19 July in Tokyo, with 1,097 Duelists who had
already qualified through Shop Qualifiers, Ranking Duel and the Duelist King Cup. Kobayashi Ryohei
won the title, beating Kosaka Kouki in the final to claim Japan's national championship and a
direct seat at Worlds. With host nation bragging rights on the line, Japan's Championship always
draws close attention heading into the World Championship itself.

## What happens next

With Oceania, North America, Europe, Korea and Japan all now settled, the World Championship field
is close to finalised ahead of the 29 to 30 August event in Tokyo. Konami runs the World
Championship across four separate titles under one roof, Trading Card Game, Duel Links Speed Duel,
Duel Links Rush Duel and Master Duel, so there's genuinely something for players across every
format Konami supports. Full qualifier lists and format details are typically confirmed on
Konami's official channels in the weeks before the event.
