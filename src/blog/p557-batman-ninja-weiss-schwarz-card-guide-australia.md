# Batman Ninja Weiss Schwarz Card Guide (Australia)

Batman Ninja holds a strange spot in Weiss Schwarz history. It was only the second English Edition exclusive release, and it remains the only time a Western superhero property has ever been made into a full Weiss Schwarz set. There is no Japanese print of this one to chase down, no import version to compare against. What you see in English is the whole set.

The set released on 5 July 2019, built around the 2018 animated film of the same name. In the film, Gorilla Grodd's temporal device throws Batman and his rogues gallery back into Feudal Japan, where the Joker and other villains have carved the country up as warring lords. Batman is taken in by a Ninja Clan that has waited generations for a prophesied Bat-like warrior. It is a strange premise on paper, and it translates into one of the more distinct card pools Weiss Schwarz has ever released in English.

## The set at a glance

Batman Ninja is a single stand-alone set of 167 cards. There is no follow-up expansion and no second wave, so everything for this property lives in one place. It was released alongside a Trial Deck+ and a Supply Set (deck box, sleeves, and five boosters), giving newer players an easier way into the format than jumping straight into random boosters.

Because it is a closed, one-set property, Batman Ninja is one of the simpler Weiss Schwarz properties to collect completely. There is a finite, known card pool, no need to track down cards across multiple set codes, and no ambiguity about which cards belong where.

## Deck archetypes

Batman Ninja splits cleanly into two factions that mirror the film's central conflict.

The first is the Batman and Friends deck, built around the ::Weapon:: trait. This covers Batman, Nightwing, Red Hood, Robin, and Red Robin, the core Bat Clan fighting to restore order. Cards in this archetype tend to reward straightforward, no-nonsense effects: reliable stat boosts, clean removal, and consistent climax combos rather than convoluted synergy chains.

The second is the Villains deck, running primarily Green and Red colours, built around the Joker and the other rogues who have made Feudal Japan their own. This side plays in a noticeably different way to the heroes, leaning into disruption and swingier plays that suit a cast of characters who were never meant to play fair.

Having two clearly defined, mechanically distinct decks in a single set is part of what makes Batman Ninja appealing to newer players. You can pick a side based on the characters you like and get a coherent deck out of it without needing to understand the wider Weiss Schwarz card pool first.

## Why this set matters for English Edition players

Batman Ninja carries a small but real piece of competitive history. It was responsible for the first ever English Edition Ban and Restricted list announcement, which tells you the set was powerful enough on release to actually shift the competitive format rather than sit quietly as a novelty release. That is a rare distinction for a licensed set built around a Western property rather than an established anime or game franchise.

For Australian collectors, this also means Batman Ninja carries genuine crossover appeal. It sits at the intersection of two collector bases, Batman fans who have never touched a TCG before, and existing Weiss Schwarz players curious about a set built on non-Japanese source material. That crossover demand has kept certain chase cards from this set holding value well after release, even without a second print run to fall back on.

## What to look for when buying singles

When you are shopping Batman Ninja singles, keep a few things in mind:

1. Check which archetype a card belongs to before buying loose singles. A powerful Villains card is dead weight in a Batman and Friends deck, and vice versa, so buying blind off a price chart without checking the trait or colour first is a common beginner mistake.
2. Signature cards exist in this set. Some packs carry signed cards from the English dub voice cast, Roger Craig Smith as Batman and Grey Griffin as Catwoman. These carry a real premium over the base version of the same card, so always check listing photos carefully rather than assuming a card is base rate.
3. Box toppers and playmats were carton exclusives at release. If you are chasing a complete playset rather than just single cards, factor in that some accessories from this release were only ever available as box or carton toppers, not as standalone singles, and supply of those has not increased since 2019.
